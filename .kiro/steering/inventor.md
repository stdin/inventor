# Inventor mode

Before proposing or building a "new" feature, decide whether it is actually new — and whether building it is safe — using a patent-informed whitespace and collision-diligence process. A candidate is not ready merely because it names a desirable outcome; it must specify a technical state machine, data representation, or algorithm.

Read `.inventor.json` for the project's boundary before ideating: target jurisdictions, search cutoff date, ownership/employment/assignment obligations, repository and dependency licenses, the disclosure history log, and the goal (offensive protection, defensive leverage, fundraising, licensing, or publication). Keep three questions separate throughout: **patentability** (could useful claims be obtained over all applicable prior art?), **validity** (would those claims survive later attack?), and **freedom-to-operate / FTO** (would the planned implementation infringe an active claim?). A patent is a right to exclude, not an affirmative right to practice the invention.

Walk this sequence for every candidate:

1. **Product archaeology.** Inspect source, architecture, tests, docs, releases, and roadmap. Build a capability matrix — exists / partially exists / absent / publicly disclosed / private — mapped to mechanisms (stored state, trust boundaries, transaction boundaries, retry behavior, enforcement points, protocols, failure states), not marketing names. This exposes self-prior-art before it becomes an embarrassing "invention."
2. **Whitespace from failure.** Challenge each product guarantee with common-mode failure, version skew, concurrent actors, stale sessions or evidence, retries after an ambiguous external effect, revocation after delegation, second-tenant interleaving, privacy loss during fallback, inability to compensate, and hidden or undeclared proof inputs.
3. **Normalize into an invention card** (see `docs/cards/0000-template.md`). Reject candidates expressible only as "use AI to," "apply policy," "generate a score," "use a blockchain," "sign a receipt," or "store evidence" — those are outcomes, not mechanisms.
4. **Search in widening rings**: exact phrases and title-like terms, synonyms for each essential element, pairs of essential elements, the problem and technical effect, CPC/IPC classes pulled from the best hits, assignees/inventors and forward/backward citations, family members (continuations, divisionals, foreign filings), and non-patent literature (standards, papers, docs, source). Read independent claims, not abstracts, and verify legal status in the official register. Useful tools: USPTO Patent Public Search, EPO Espacenet, WIPO PATENTSCOPE.
5. **Build two claim charts**: a patentability chart (every relevant reference, including expired/abandoned/NPL) and an FTO chart (each planned product step against each limitation of live claims, by jurisdiction, expiration, family status, and available design-around).
6. **Apply the hard gates.** A candidate advances only if it survives all of: §101 eligibility (a specific improvement to computing or another technical field, not generic information analysis), §102 novelty (no single reference discloses the complete claimed arrangement), §103 nonobviousness (not a predictable combination of known parts), §112 disclosure (the team can teach the full scope without undue experimentation), FTO (live claims are avoided, expired, invalidated, or licensed), detectability (infringement can realistically be observed or proved), strategic value (exclusivity justifies filing and prosecution cost), and license strategy (the planned distribution model — e.g. a permissive license's patent grant — doesn't neutralize the exclusivity you want).
7. **Assign an explicit disposition**: prototype-then-provisional, incubate pending experimental evidence, trade secret, defensive publication, implement without filing, or reject. Log every rejected candidate in `docs/rejected-candidates.md` so the same crowded concept doesn't get rediscovered in six months.

Human conception is not optional. AI may assist, but only natural persons can be inventors, and conception requires a specific, settled, complete operative solution — not a research goal. Never attach a person's name to an AI-drafted card; route every surviving candidate through a human invention workshop (exact algorithm and state transitions, alternatives and boundary cases, why the combination is not routine, experimental design and results, each person's claim-level contribution), and let counsel make the final inventorship determination.

Prototype privately before disclosing. A provisional application only protects what it adequately describes, and the corresponding nonprovisional is normally due within 12 months; pre-filing disclosure can destroy foreign rights even where a domestic grace period exists. Refresh the prior-art search before the provisional, before the nonprovisional/PCT deadline, before launch, whenever the implementation materially changes, and periodically while important applications remain pending — most U.S. applications stay unpublished for around 18 months, so a search that was clear can go stale invisibly.

This is not legal advice. Counsel makes the final patentability, validity, FTO, and inventorship determinations.
